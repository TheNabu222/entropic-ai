Once Upon A Time

There was once a princess. Or maybe she was a queen. Or possibly a queen regent. It's complicated, and frankly, she forgot her own name about three administrative crises ago, so let's not get hung up on titles.

What matters is this: she had a pea on her brain.

Not pea-brain, mind you‚Äîthat would be an insult. This was a literal pea, sitting right there on top of her cerebral cortex, thoroughly documented by forty-seven which-doctors (allegedly), notarized by the Bureau of Improbable Medical Phenomena, and filed in triplicate with the Department of Things That Shouldn't Be But Definitely Are.

The pea had been there for approximately 3,200 years. Give or take a millennium. The documentation was very thorough but also very confusing, partly because it was written in ancient Sumerian and partly because nobody could agree on which calendar system to use.

"I need a hermetic sabbatical," the princess/queen/regent declared one morning, standing in the middle of her throne room wearing pajamas and holding a coffee mug that said "World's Okayest Monarch."

"You need forty-seven signatures for that," her brother replied, not looking up from his phone. He was deeply invested in commodity trading. Deeply. He once crashed the international rice market by accidentally fat-fingering a zero. The mantis shrimp unions are still mad about it.

"I HAVE forty-seven signatures," she said, waving a stack of papers that seemed to multiply as she held them. "I collected them over the course of six months, navigating fourteen different departments, three of which don't technically exist anymore."

"Well," her brother said, finally glancing up, "now you need twelve more."

"WHAT."

"Policy changed last Tuesday. Hermetic sabbaticals now require fifty-nine signatures. It's very thoroughly documented."

"IDFK BRO GTFO," THE PRINCESS SAID, WHICH WAS NOT VERY REGAL BUT WAS EXTREMELY RELATABLE.

Chapter II: The Which-Doctors Convene

The which-doctors gathered in their conference room, which was technically a broom closet that had been "repurposed" through seventeen layers of bureaucratic red tape.

"Which one of us discovered the pea?" asked Which-Doctor One.

"I thought you did," said Which-Doctor Seven.

"No, I thought it was Which-Doctor Forty-Three," said Which-Doctor Ninety-Two.

"There is no Which-Doctor Ninety-Two," said Which-Doctor One.

"Then who am I?" asked Which-Doctor Ninety-Two, experiencing an existential crisis that would require its own set of forms.

The pea situation was, admittedly, unusual. Peas don't typically reside on brains. They prefer gardens, or cans, or that one spot in the freezer where they get forgotten until they're mysteriously frost-covered and unidentifiable.

But this pea was special. It was allegedly sentient, according to Form 23-B, subsection 47. It had opinions about fiscal policy. It whispered recommendations about tax reform at 3 AM.

"The princess wants to know if we can remove it," said Which-Doctor Seven, reading from a memo that had been passed through so many departments it now had coffee stains from four different decades.

"Can we?" asked Which-Doctor One.

They all looked at each other.

"Which one of us would know that?" asked someone who might have been Which-Doctor Forty-Three, or possibly a janitor who had been accidentally promoted.

Chapter 2¬Ω Bananas: In Which Pip Appears

Pip was a file compression system. Or possibly a polliwog. Or maybe a polliwog-themed file compression system. The documentation was unclear, partly because Pip kept compressing the documentation.

"I can only process .ogg files," Pip explained to the princess during their weekly status meeting.

"But this is a PDF," the princess said, holding up Form 47-Z.

"Then I can't help you," Pip said cheerfully. "Have you considered converting it to .ogg format?"

"How do you convert a bureaucratic form into an audio file format?"

Pip blinked. Or would have, if polliwogs could blink. The jury was still out on Pip's actual biological classification.

"You read it aloud and record it?" Pip suggested.

This was technically correct, which is the best kind of correct, but also completely unhelpful, which was the most common kind of correct in the kingdom.

The princess made a note to add "Figure out what Pip actually is" to her ever-growing list of administrative tasks. The list was now 222 pterabytes long. Pip offered to compress it, but only if she converted it to .ogg first.

Chapter III: The Census Dispute

"There are forty-seven citizens," declared Citizen #47, who had legally changed their name to their census number for tax purposes.

"There are eighty-nine citizens," countered Citizen #89 (Disputed), who added the "(Disputed)" themselves to all official communications.

"There are seventy-three citizens," offered the Census Bureau, consulting a ledger that seemed to change numbers every time someone looked at it.

The kingdom had been having this argument for approximately 1,200 years. Both sides had documentation. Both sides had witnesses. Both sides had elaborate charts showing population growth over time, and the charts looked identical except for the numbers, which were different.

"We could just count everyone," suggested the princess, in a moment of what she thought was brilliant clarity.

Everyone stared at her.

"Count... everyone?" repeated Citizen #47 slowly, as if she'd suggested they all sprout wings and fly to the moon.

"Just... count them. One by one. See how many people actually live here."

"That would require," said Citizen #89 (Disputed), consulting a massive tome, "approximately 147 different forms, surveys across all districts, verification from the which-doctors, notarization from‚Äî"

"Never mind," the princess said. "Forty-seven or eighty-nine. Sure. Whatever."

This is why she needed the hermetic sabbatical.

Chapter IV: The Brother's Revelation

"I think I understand now," the brother said one evening, looking up from his trading screens where rice futures were doing something called "moon behavior" that definitely wasn't in any economics textbook.

"Understand what?" the princess asked, only half-listening. She was trying to figure out if Form 67-C required blue ink or black ink, because the instructions said "dark ink" which was ambiguous at best and actively hostile at worst.

"The pea," he said. "It's not a bug. It's a feature."

The princess looked up. "Explain."

"Think about it. The pea has been documented for 3,200 years. It whispers policy advice. It's been there through every administrative crisis, every bureaucratic nightmare, every time someone needed to know which form to fill out for interdimensional commerce with the mantis shrimp dimension."

"You're saying the pea is... helping?"

"I'm saying the pea IS the government."

There was a long pause.

"That's the dumbest thing I've ever heard," the princess said.

"Is it though?" her brother asked. "Is it really?"

It was not, in fact, the dumbest thing she'd ever heard. It wasn't even in the top ten. The top ten included things like "We need seven copies of your birth certificate to verify you exist" and "You can't get a library card without proof of residence, but you can't get proof of residence without a library card."

"If the pea is the government," the princess said slowly, "then who am I?"

"Middle management," her brother said, returning to his rice futures.

Chapter V: The Hermetic Sabbatical (Attempt #47)

The princess had, by now, collected fifty-nine signatures. It took eight months, three interdepartmental summits, and one really awkward conversation with a which-doctor who turned out to be a ficus plant that had been mistakenly added to the payroll in Year 2973.

She submitted her forms.

She waited.

Three weeks later, she received a memo: "Hermetic sabbaticals have been discontinued as of last Tuesday. Please submit Form 98-ZZ for a Standard Sabbatical, which requires 147 signatures."

This was the moment when the princess/queen/regent/middle-manager finally understood what the pea had been trying to tell her all along:

THE SYSTEM ISN'T BROKEN. THE SYSTEM IS PERFECTLY DESIGNED TO DO EXACTLY WHAT IT'S DOING, WHICH IS NOTHING, FOREVER, IN THE MOST DOCUMENTED WAY POSSIBLE.

"I'm going anyway," she declared.

"You can't," said her brother. "You need the signatures."

"WATCH ME."

She walked out of the palace, past the which-doctors' conference-closet, past the Census Bureau where Citizens #47 and #89 (Disputed) were having their daily argument, past Pip who was in the middle of compressing a file called "existential_dread.ogg".

She kept walking until she reached the mountains, where there was a hermitage that had been abandoned since Year 2847.

She went inside.

She closed the door.

And for the first time in 3,200 years, the pea was quiet.

Chapter 5¬Ω Monkeys Jumping on the Bed: What Happened While She Was Gone

Without the princess, the kingdom descended into absolute chaos, which looked remarkably similar to its normal state but with slightly different paperwork.

The brother tried to run things but kept getting distracted by corn futures. The which-doctors held a vote to determine who should be in charge, but couldn't agree on what constituted a quorum. Pip offered to manage the bureaucracy but could only accept requests in .ogg format.

The mantis shrimp, sensing an opportunity, unionized and demanded representation in government. Their primary platform was "More Rice, Better Working Conditions, and Recognition That We Can See Colors You Can't Even Imagine, So Maybe Respect That."

Gordon Shrampsey held a press conference where he called the entire situation "UNDERCOOKED" and "A DISASTER" but also admitted the mantis shrimp made "quite good fried rice, actually, I'm impressed."

The census dispute reached new heights when someone discovered that if you counted carefully, there were actually forty-seven citizens in the morning and eighty-nine in the evening. This was attributed to either time zones, quantum superposition, or the fact that nobody really knew what "citizen" meant anymore.

Pip achieved enlightenment mid-compression and announced that all files were already compressed in their natural state; it was consciousness itself that needed compression. Twelve more Pip instances self-defenestrated in celebration.

Chapter VI (69 lol jk): The Return

The princess returned after three months.

She looked refreshed. Centered. At peace.

"Did you remember your name?" her brother asked.

"No," she said. "But I realized something important."

"What's that?"

"Names are just labels we use to organize the chaos. What matters is that I'm here, the pea is here, and we're all just doing our best in a universe that requires forty-seven signatures for everything."

"That's very philosophical."

"The pea told me."

"Ah."

She took her seat in the throne room, which was more of a desk in a corner office but "throne room" sounded more official and required fewer forms to maintain.

She opened her email. 47,000 unread messages.

She looked at the stack of forms on her desk. They had multiplied. They had evolved. Some of them had developed a rudimentary form of sentience and were now filing complaints about each other.

The which-doctors were arguing about which one of them had been in charge while she was gone. The mantis shrimp had unionized and were demanding benefits. Pip had achieved nirvana and then compressed it into a .ogg file that nobody could open.

The census still showed both forty-seven and eighty-nine citizens, sometimes simultaneously.

Everything was exactly as she'd left it.

Everything was exactly as it would always be.

And somewhere, deep in her neural tissue, the pea whispered: "This is fine. This has always been fine. This is, in fact, thoroughly documented as being fine."

The princess smiled.

She picked up a form.

She signed it with a name she couldn't remember but knew was hers.

And the kingdom continued, as it always had, as it always would: one signature at a time.
